Thermal study of a re-entry space launcher
An in-depth exploration of heat distribution on a re-entry rocket, modeled using the Finite Element Method and variational formulation. This project blends theoretical mathematics and numerical matrix computations to solve partial differential equations describing thermal behavior. Implemented with FreeFEM and Python.
Project Details / Background
During my first-year master’s project, I studied the thermal behavior inside a rocket's astronaut capsule during atmospheric re-entry. The objective was to model and simulate the temperature distribution $u(x)$ within the rocket structure by solving a partial differential equation that describes heat propagation with mixed boundary conditions (Neumann, Dirichlet, and Fourier-Robin).
I learned to code numerical matrix analysis in Python, where I implemented finite element discretizations and solved large sparse linear systems resulting from the variational formulation of the PDE. The variational formulation was done using FreeFEM, a specialized C-based software for solving PDEs with finite elements.
The core PDE system was:
$$ \left\{ \begin{array}{ll} -\nabla \cdot (K_a \nabla u) = f(x, y) & \text{in } \Omega \\\\ -K_a \frac{\partial u}{\partial \vec{n}} = 0 & \text{on } \Gamma_N \quad \text{(Neumann condition)} \\\\ -K_a \frac{\partial u}{\partial \vec{n}} = \alpha (u - u_E) & \text{on } \Gamma_F \quad \text{(Fourier-Robin condition)} \\\\ u = u_D & \text{on } \Gamma_D \quad \text{(Dirichlet condition)} \end{array} \right. $$
This system models heat propagation within the rocket’s structure during atmospheric re-entry. Here, $K_a$ denotes the thermal conductivity of air (e.g., $K_a = 0.026 \,\mathrm{W \cdot m^{-1} \cdot K^{-1}}$), $f(x, y)$ is an internal heat source such as air conditioning, and $\alpha$ is the heat transfer coefficient on the convective boundary $\Gamma_F$. The Fourier-Robin condition was particularly useful in simulating convective heat exchange with the environment. I showed that as $\alpha$ increases, the condition approximates a Dirichlet boundary.
I formulated the problem variationally and discretized it into a linear system $AU = F$, where $A$ is symmetric positive definite and sparse. This structure enabled the use of efficient solvers such as the Cholesky decomposition and the conjugate gradient method.
To better understand the physical phenomena and the sensitivity of the system, I performed simulations by varying parameters such as the thickness of the thermal shield, the speed of the rocket, and the external temperature. These simulations allowed me to identify which parameters most strongly influence the internal temperature distribution and the effectiveness of the thermal shield.
This project was my first opportunity to combine advanced mathematical modeling, numerical linear algebra, and programming skills to solve a real-world engineering problem in aerospace. It helped me solidify my understanding of PDEs, variational methods, and numerical analysis. The work culminated in a comprehensive report of over 80 pages detailing the theoretical and technical aspects of the analysis.
